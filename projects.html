Current Projects

Vehicle-Driver Interaction for Automated Vehicle takeovers

This work utilizes a driving simulator, interview, and STM analysis. The premise of this study is as follows:

We know that automated vehicles can drive extremely well by themselves, but they often don't drive in a way that makes the driver feel
safe or happy. We want the car to drive itself as much as possible because we know AV technology is safer and the
future of our roads. So we set up many small trials, each with an obstacle or slightly annoying aspect in different weather
conditions, give the car - which drives perfectly safe - the freedom to drive, and observe the participant. The participant has a game
to play on the side, which provides a monetary incentive for playing it. Now that the driver is adequately distracted, we run the trials
and see when they stop playing the game and take over control of the vehicle. We also utilize different takeover techniques, including
different button presses and notification screens. We then perform an interview, transcribe, and run through R's STM package to determine,
as unbaisedly as possible, the common themes for each scenario/question. A lot going on but we plan to submit this work to Transportation C's
call for papers.

Past Projects

Machine Learning, Natural Language Processing, Computer Vision

In Abu Dhabi, I worked with my PI Chih-Jen Lin from National Taiwan University & RA Yu-Chen who is now at Carnegie Melon. My sub project
was research into long document text classification and I worked with Osama from Morocco. We had a blast utilizing BERT, heirarchical
BERT, and SVMs on new datasets we developed and preprocessed. We found that SVMs worked significantly better for this task - taking
significantly less resources in both time and computing power. Being able to utilize CPU with SVMs means this classification problem is more
inclusive, as you don't need to outsource all the work to expensive GPUs. SVMs were less variable compared to its deep learning counterparts,
providing great baseline performances to build & test new models on. This was a once in a lifetime opportunity and I had a blast, I hope to
return to the middle east soon!

BlackJack

Still my all-time favoite project. In highschool, I made a virtual BlackJack game with a betting system where the user
plays against the dealer. This game, made entirely in Java, was my first introduction to the possibilities of Computer Science.
I had just taken the AP Computer Science exam and thought that those ridiculous questions were what Computer Scientists did
day-to-day, but after making this game I knew it was something I wanted to pursue in some way. It actually was quite realistic,
the dealer would pull another card if it had less than the user (only if the user was at 21 or under of course), and the betting
system was quite realistic as well. Never got to implement splitting or anything like that, but I would always end up losing all
my "credits" anyways so I knew it worked and was realistic. Unfortunately, this project has been lost to the dark depths of the
dumpster when my highschool decided to spend $3.2 quadrillion to redo the whole school, including changing computers and data centers.

Eagle Scout Project

This project brought my community a new campsite that taught people how to low-impact camp. Working with the Ice Age Trail Association
this was built as an official campsite that still serves visitors today. This camp adopts the principals of Leave No Trace, ideals
Scouts hold dear. This campsite has tent pads outside of the "Bear-muda triangle" - a term deemed to the area between the cooking
area, sump, and bear bag areas. The campsite also has a sign with infografics and explanations of the camp, as well as a traffic log.

This project taught me valuable project management skills such as planning and executing multiple work days, working with local
companies to source materials (sign, sump), communicating between administrative bodies (Wisconsin DNR, Ice Age Trail Association),
and delivering on time. I also learned valuable handy man skills, all those posts, frames, and sumps don't build themselves.
